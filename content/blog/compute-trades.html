The inference compute bottleneck is the defining constraint of 2026–2030. Here's how to think about allocating capital across the stack — from EUV machines to token orchestration.

Not financial advice · First-principles analysis · Updated March 2026

The core thesis is simple: margin flows to whoever controls the tightest bottleneck. That bottleneck is shifting — from power (2024–25) to logic+memory (2026–27) to EUV lithography (2028+). The goal is to be positioned where the bottleneck is moving to, not where it currently is.

Why does the bottleneck keep shifting?

Each layer of the AI supply chain has a different lead time. Data centers: 8–12 months. Power: 1–2 years. Fabs: 2–3 years. EUV tools: 3–5 years. As the shorter-lead-time constraints get solved (power and data centers are being built rapidly), the binding constraint cascades down to the layers that take longest to scale.

Per Dylan Patel: there's no more capacity to slide from mobile/PC to AI. Nvidia is now the largest customer at both TSMC and SK Hynix. The consumer-to-AI shift is fully tapped out. From here, scaling means building new fab capacity — and that's gated by ASML's ability to produce EUV tools.

What's the ASML hard ceiling?

ASML currently makes ~70 EUV tools per year at $300–400M each. Even under aggressive expansion, they reach only ~100/year by decade's end. The cumulative installed base will be ~700 tools. At 3.5 EUV tools per gigawatt of Rubin-class chips, that's a theoretical ceiling of ~200 GW/year — if every single tool were allocated to AI, which it won't be.

For context, there's ~20 GW of AI deployed in the world right now. Sam Altman wants a gigawatt per week by 2030 (~52 GW/year). Even the most optimistic EUV math can't support the ambitions of all the major labs simultaneously. This is the binding constraint of the decade.

02 — The GPU appreciation thesis

Hardware that gets more valuable over time

The most counterintuitive insight from the Patel interview

Why is an H100 worth more today than at launch?

The conventional bear case says GPUs depreciate as better chips arrive. But in a supply-constrained world, the price of a GPU isn't set by what else you could buy — it's set by the value you can extract from it today.

GPT-5.4 runs cheaper per token and produces higher-quality output than GPT-4 on the same hardware. The TAM for GPT-4 tokens was maybe tens of billions. For GPT-5.4 tokens, it's north of $100B. The same H100 is now producing more valuable output. Ergo: the GPU is worth more.

Labs are signing H100 deals at $2.40/hr for 2–3 years. The TCO to build a Hopper cluster across five years is ~$1.40/hr. That's 70%+ gross margin for whoever locked in early. Michael Burry's depreciation thesis is simply wrong in a world where you can't build enough of the next-gen chip to meet demand.

What's the Alchian-Allen effect on model pricing?

If GPUs get more expensive via a fixed-cost increase, the ratio between using the best model vs. a mediocre one narrows. If an H100 goes from $2 to $3/hr, but Opus produces 1M tokens and Sonnet produces 2M, the effective premium for frontier quality shrinks from 2x to 1.5x. Everyone shifts to the best model.

Investment implication: The compute crunch creates a flywheel for whoever has the best model. Best model → most revenue → can pay highest prices for scarce compute → locks in more capacity → maintains best model. This is why "all the volumes are on the best models today."

03 — The foundry diversification trade

TSMC, Samsung, and the $73B question

Ben Thompson's TSMC Risk thesis meets Samsung's counter-offensive

Is Samsung a real alternative to TSMC?

Wall Street has hated Samsung Foundry for three years — yield disasters, customer defections, market share erosion to 7.3%. But the structural setup is changing rapidly.

The bull case: Samsung's SF2 2nm GAA process has hit 55–60% yields. Tesla signed a $16.5B foundry contract — the largest long-term deal ever with a single client. Meta is considering Samsung for MTIA accelerators. AMD for EPYC Venice and MI450. TSMC's 2nm wafers cost $30K+; Samsung offers $22–25K. Samsung is investing $73B in 2026 alone.

The bear case: Real design wins below 4nm are still scarce. Most Samsung wins are at 7nm. TSMC is spending 5x more on foundry than Samsung. The "AMD and Apple using Samsung" rumor surfaces every year and never materializes.

The Thompson argument: The opportunity cost of being chip-constrained at decade's end will vastly exceed whatever it costs now to make Samsung or Intel viable. Hyperscalers have to diversify — not because Samsung is better, but because TSMC is sold out and the risk of single-supplier dependency is existential for the AI buildout.

What about Intel Foundry?

Intel Foundry is the wildcard. Bleeding $2.5B/quarter. The 18A node is the make-or-break moment. If yields work by mid-2026, Intel becomes the second viable alternative and dilutes Samsung's "only option" narrative. Aschenbrenner held Intel call options while dumping the equity — maximum upside exposure, minimum dead capital. That's the right structure: cheap optionality on a turnaround.

04 — The memory crunch

HBM is the next bottleneck to bite

Enormous and underappreciated

How bad is the memory shortage?

HBM consumes 4x the wafer area per gigabyte versus standard DRAM. Its share of total DRAM output is rising to 23% in 2026 and will keep climbing. This crowds out consumer memory — Micron already exited its Crucial consumer brand entirely. SK Group's chair warned the conventional memory shortage could last 4–5 years.

A single gigawatt of Rubin chips requires ~170,000 DRAM wafers. Memory vendors are doubling or tripling prices again while locking in multi-year contracts. Nvidia has $90B in long-term supply contracts and is negotiating three-year deals with memory vendors now.

The trade: SK Hynix (HBM leader), Samsung Memory (catching up, tied to the $73B investment), and Micron (third player). Memory is where the margin is migrating as logic wafer allocation gets locked up years ahead.

05 — Power: solvable but lucrative

The energy layer

The trade has worked spectacularly. The question is what's left.

Is power still a good trade?

Patel's reframe is important: doubling power costs adds only ~$0.10/hr to GPU TCO. Power is a cost, not a constraint. Data centers are being built in 8 months. 56 GW across 46 behind-the-meter projects have been announced. One-third of data centers may be fully off-grid by 2030.

But the trade has already been spectacular. Vistra +321% in 2024. Constellation Energy tripled. Oklo +700%. Aschenbrenner's fund has Bloom Energy as its single largest holding, with heavy positions in EQT, Solaris, and the crypto-miner-to-AI-hosting pivots (CORZ, IREN, Riot, Hut 8, BitDeer).

The nuance: Power is still a great business — everyone building AI needs it, and permitting creates durable moats. But the asymmetry is less extreme than in the semiconductor layer. The biggest energy gains are probably behind us unless you're buying optionality on the truly long-duration buildout (2028+ data center capacity that's being contracted for now).

06 — The demand accelerant

Token orchestration, not token selling

From Semi-analysis: the future is orchestrating tokens, not selling them at base cost

Why does the demand layer matter for infrastructure investors?

Claude Code is at 4% of GitHub commits today, expected to reach 20% by year-end. Autonomous task horizons are doubling every 6–7 months. Each doubling unlocks more of the information-work TAM. The agentic layer — tools, memory, sub-agents, verification loops — creates compounding inference demand that makes every infrastructure layer below it more valuable.

As Eric Jang wrote: "I don't think people have begun to fathom how much inference compute we will need. Even if you think you are AGI-pilled, I think you are still underestimating how starved of compute we will be."

The key transition from the Semi-analysis thesis: ChatGPT/API was Web 1.0 — static request/response. Claude Code and agents are Web 2.0 — dynamic orchestration. The protocol (raw token generation) becomes the means, not the end. The trillions in value will be in the orchestration layer. But that layer's growth is throttled by every bottleneck above it.

07 — The short side

What gets disrupted

Per-seat software, traditional IT services, and the MSFT conundrum

What's structurally impaired?

Per-seat SaaS: Microsoft's conundrum is definitional. They need to accelerate Azure (which arms competitors) while defending Office 365 (which agents are making obsolete). Why standardize Salesforce if an agent just queries the database directly? SF is a form-and-workflow wrapper — both can be scaffolded by AI. The three SaaS moats — data switching costs, workflow lock-in, integration complexity — are all partially eroded at the margins.

IT services: Aschenbrenner is short Infosys. The deflationary cost of intelligence reprices every info company's margin for repeatable work. Accenture's largest Claude Code deployment (30K employees) is simultaneously the trade working and the admission that the old model is dying.

MSFT specifically: 365 Copilot has 15M paying customers — tiny vs. total 365 base. It's $30/user/month on top of $35–57/user/month for E3/E5. The per-seat model is built for humans clicking buttons. Agents don't need seats. Microsoft wiped $357B in a single session when this reality priced in. They're trying to win at both cloud rental and productivity software — "renting capacity to barbarians who will ruin their castle."

Key allocation principles

Weight toward the moving bottleneck. ~35% in the semiconductor layer (ASML + TSMC + Samsung + memory) because that's where the constraint is tightening. ~18% in chip designers (Nvidia + Broadcom) because they've already locked in allocation. ~16% in infrastructure (neoclouds + networking + power). ~8% in optionality (Intel calls + index calls). 5% short basket.

Match time horizon to bottleneck timing. Power and neocloud positions can be shorter duration (benefiting now). Foundry and memory positions are 2026–28 plays. ASML is the longest-duration hold — the constraint that tightens last but matters most.

Use options where conviction is binary. Intel (turnaround or bust), index calls (AGI acceleration or not). Defined loss, asymmetric upside. This is the Aschenbrenner structure.

Where SA and Compute Trades converge

Physical infrastructure > algorithms. SA holds zero model company equity and zero pure software. Every dollar is in the physical substrate: power, data centers, networking, memory, chips. Our framework agrees — margin flows to bottleneck owners, not to the companies spending on the bottleneck.

The memory bet is accelerating. SA's 816% increase in SanDisk confirms the memory crunch thesis. OpenAI has reportedly committed to consume up to 40% of global DRAM capacity. SanDisk gross margins hit 51% in Q2 2026, up 29% YoY. This validates our memory allocation — and suggests we may be underweight.

Optical networking is a quiet conviction play. Lumentum ($479M) and Coherent ($89M) are SA's 4th and 11th largest positions. We had this at 5%. The data movement problem scales with cluster size — as we go from 1 GW to 5+ GW clusters, optical interconnect demand compounds non-linearly.

The Intel structure is instructive. Selling 20.2M common shares to exactly 1 while keeping 20.2M call options is a masterclass in capital efficiency. Frees hundreds of millions for redeployment while maintaining full upside. This is how to play binary outcomes — options, not equity.

Where SA and Compute Trades diverge — and what it means

SA has no ASML, no TSMC, no Samsung Foundry. The most notable gap. Zero direct exposure to the EUV / foundry layer that Patel identifies as the 2028+ ultimate constraint. SA exited TSM puts and holds no foundry equity. Our framework allocates 35% here. SA likely thinks the foundry layer is correctly priced while infra is mispriced — a shorter time horizon bet.

SA has no Nvidia. Zero direct exposure. Exited all puts too. Our framework has 12%. SA may be betting that margin migrates from chip designers toward infrastructure providers — or that Nvidia is fairly valued while the neoclouds are not.

SA is 43% neoclouds + BTC miners. CoreWeave ($1.2B), Core Scientific ($419M, 9.4% ownership), IREN ($329M), Applied Digital ($278M), Cipher ($155M). Our framework has 13%. SA makes a far more aggressive bet that GPU hosting margins expand as older chips appreciate. The Patel depreciation thesis directly supports this — but it's the highest-conviction, highest-risk layer.

The leverage question. SA's ADV shows $383M AUM but $5.5B in 13F exposure — roughly 14x gross leverage. As one hedge fund PM noted in the comments on the Capitalist analysis: 13F is not AUM, it's gross exposure excluding shorts. SA is probably massively levered. Directionally brilliant, but structurally fragile if the thesis is even slightly wrong on timing. For our framework, this reinforces diversification across the stack rather than concentration.

The adoption timing risk SA is absorbing

The Capitalist piece raises a sharp counterpoint: ~90% of businesses have not adopted AI meaningfully (RBA data). OpenAI's revenue growth slowed to ~17% from Nov 2025 to March 2026, making their $280B 2030 target look ambitious. If revenue inflection doesn't come fast enough to justify the capex buildout, the neoclouds are the most exposed — they're sitting on commoditized data center capacity with potentially obsolete chips.

SA's average multiple across top 5 positions is ~14x EV/Revenue. This is not value investing. It's pricing in exponentials. Leopold's edge — asymmetric information from being embedded in SF's AI ecosystem — is real. But it's also the same information that priced these stocks up in the first place.

Our key takeaway: The semiconductor layer (ASML, TSMC, memory vendors) has pricing power regardless of whether AI demand materializes as fast as the labs hope. They're sold out either way. The infra layer (neoclouds, power) needs demand to materialize on schedule. This is why our framework weights semiconductors more heavily — it's the more robust position against the timing risk SA is absorbing.

Net effect: portfolio tilts further toward the semiconductor supply chain and data-movement layer, while acknowledging SA's strong signals on memory (SNDK) and optical networking (LITE, COHR).

11 — What breaks the thesis

Risk factors

When does this trade stop working?

Demand collapse: If AI models stop improving or adoption stalls, the entire capex cycle looks like overbuilding. This is the dot-com scenario. Patel's counter: "We're not even close to the dot-com bubble" — the revenue is real ($60B+ Anthropic trajectory, Nvidia $216B rev).

Algorithmic efficiency gains: If models get dramatically more efficient (e.g., 10x fewer FLOPs for same quality), demand for hardware could plateau even as capability grows. Watch for architectural breakthroughs that reduce compute requirements faster than demand grows.

Taiwan escalation: China's Five-Year Plan language on Taiwan has shifted. A blockade or attack on TSMC would be catastrophic for the entire thesis except power/energy names. TSMC Arizona (8 fabs planned, ~30% of advanced production at scale) is the hedge but won't be ready this decade.

Regulatory intervention: Antitrust action against hyperscaler capex, export controls that shift, or energy policy changes could alter the landscape. Currently the US government is encouraging the buildout, not constraining it.

Concentration risk: This portfolio is extremely correlated to one macro thesis. If the thesis is wrong — if inference demand curves flatten — everything moves against you simultaneously. Size accordingly.
